LEARNING THROUGH BODY POSITION AND MOVEMENT - PROPRIOCEPTION

OVER responsive state-
over-registering sensory information, responding with sensitivity, avoiding, defensiveness, fight/flight/fright or freeze
Note: It is rare to find a person with sensitivity to proprioceptive input. Most people seek it out as a feel-good sensation and regulating tool. Proprioceptive input through heavy muscle work is the most powerful strategy for most students with sensory processing challenges.

This student may:
AFSN
Avoid deep pressure (hugs, massage or firm touch) (note: this could be a tactile/touch issue rather than a proprioceptive issue).
Avoid activities that involve heavy muscle work (this could be due to poor strength or coordination of movement rather than sensitivity to proprioceptive input).
Avoid or pull away if joints are squeezed together or compressed (e.g. avoid carrying heavy items or push/pull activities - however, this could be due to poor strength or coordination of movement)

UNDER responsive state -
low registration of sensory input, slow or low response, "zoned out"
Note: Your student's responses in this category may be due to poor strength or coordination of movement. Tick the boxes that apply below and refer to the Meaningful Movement Module for more information.

This student may:
AFSN
Apply light pressure on pencil, be aimless when drawing, make light, unidentifiable marks on page.
Let the pencil or object fall out of his or her hand and not notice.
Have difficulty imitating or copying body positions or actions.
Lean on people or furniture.
Slump when sitting / have poor posture/ seem floppy or weak (possibly due to having low muscle tone).
Sit on floor with knees bent and legs in a W-position (feet beside bottom)
Trip or fall frequently or bump into objects or people.
Fail to catch themselves or stop themselves from falling.
Show poor coordination and appear to be clumsy.
Seem unaware of his/her own actions or impact on the environment (e.g. didn't notice that he knocked over an object).

Seeking the zone -
seeking/craving sensory input to calm down or increase alertness or to maintain a focussed state

This student may:
AFSN
Seek out heavy muscle work (climb, push, pull, lift, drag).
Craves movement and input to muscles and joints (e.g. stomp, walk heavily, run, bounce, jump).
Crave deep pressure touch, squeeze, squash, leaning against others (this can be calming and organising input).
Seek out tight, squashed space
Move furniture, lift and carry objects
Seem to enjoy bumping into people or objects or purposely fall
Grasp objects too tightly.
Use too much force or be rough, break objects and seem destructive without meaning to be (e.g. slams door)
Walk on tip toes and/or lock knee joints to stiffen legs and get more stability and control through legs (also in tactile and vestibular sections)

This checklist is a guide for teachers. Please consult an Occupational Therapist specialising in sensory processing challenges for formal assessment and intervention of your students with complex needs.

LEARNING THROUGH TOUCH -TACTILE PROCESSING

OVER responsive state-
over-registering sensory information, responding with sensitivity, avoiding, defensiveness, fight/flight/fright or freeze

This student may:
AFSN
Avoid touching different textures or participating in activities such as craft, messy play, cooking or gardening, be wary, hesitant or distressed.
Appear anxious to clean hands or face or change clothes if wet or messy.
Over-react to light touch (e.g. another student brushing past or touching them or tickling. However this student may seek out deep pressure or potentially painful sensations)
Pull away from being touched or refuse physical comfort (may need own space and time to recover rather than hugs or touch).
Resist hand-over-hand guidance.
Dislikes/ avoids being too close to other students or teachers, may be restless or disruptive when lining up or sitting close to other students.
Prefers touch on his or her own terms (prefers to be in control and initiate touch).
Be fussy about clothing, rip or tear clothing if distressed or irritated (E.g. may only wear long sleeves even in summer or need tags removed from clothing).
Walk on tip toes to limit tactile input to feet, avoid walking barefoot or be fussy about wearing socks and shoes.

UNDER responsive state -
low registration of sensory input, slow or low response, "zoned out"

This student may:
AFSN
Be slow to process tactile information or touch cues.
Not notice food on face or mess on hands.
Not notice when underpants or nappy is soiled or wet, unaware of bowel or bladder motions.
Not notice that they are holding an item in their hands.
Find it difficult to locate items in pocket or bag using touch alone (may need to look).
Not notice clothes twisted or ill-fitting.
Appear to have poor pencil grasp.
Apply light pressure on pencil.
Doesn't seem to feel pain (e.g. slow to react to pain, be unaware of cuts and bruises).
Doesn't seem to notice temperature (e.g. wears t-shirt in cold weather or jacket in summer).

Seeking the zone -
seeking/craving sensory input to calm down or increase alertness or to maintain a focussed state

This student may:
AFSN
Have a strong need to touch furniture, walls, objects, people.
Crave certain tactile input and textures (e.g. water, messy activities, bark, twigs, sand, plays with food).
Seek out hot or cold surfaces (e.g. the window, fridge or metal objects).
Smear faeces or play with wet nappy.
Frequently go to the bathroom or bubbler to seek out water.
Crave touch, hugs or physical affection.
Resort to self-harm (hit or bite own body, fall on floor) and not seem to find it painful.
Frequently bump into people or objects on purpose.
Fidget-flick, twirl, touch objects or people excessively (if looking intently, they may be craving visual and vestibular input).
Handle objects in a rough manner, e.g. bangs, pulls, pushes, breaks toys unintentionally.
Like to walk barefoot and explore textures and surfaces with feet.

This checklist is a guide for teachers. Please consult an Occupational Therapist specialising in sensory processing challenges for formal assessment and intervention of your students with complex needs.

LEARNING THROUGH MOVEMENT -VESTIBULAR PROCESSING

OVER responsive state-
over-registering sensory information, responding with sensitivity, avoiding, defensiveness, fight/flight/fright or freeze

This student may:
AFSN
Fear being upside down, tipped sideways, being off balance or when moving backwards (this applies to a student using a wheelchair who may startle when pushed forward or back without warning or at a fast, sudden pace).
Become anxious when feet are not touching the ground (e.g. sitting on the toilet or chair with feet unsupported).
Become anxious or avoids slides, swings, spinning, jumping or even dancing
Be anxious about walking up or down inclines or stairs.
Be afraid of movement, fear falling or heights, may avoid balance activities.
Over-react to small changes in position (e.g. if slightly off balance or stumbles).
Get motion sickness easily.
Become overwhelmed and flighty when out in an open space (e.g. an oval). May appear to be running away (similar item in seeking in the zone. This could also be related to a fear of open spaces- agoraphobia or visual sensitivity).
Need to be in control of his/her own movement.
Startle easily or freeze in shock or drop to ground in fright if unbalanced, bumped or moved without warning.
Be afraid of or avoid busy environments where he/she may get jostled or knocked over.

UNDER responsive state -
low registration of sensory input, slow or low response, "zoned out"

This student may:
AFSN
Appears not to feel dizzy (e.g. Can spin and swing more than others without being unsteady after or showing rapid eye movement after (Post Rotary Nystagmus)).
Experience difficulty maintaining balance and controlling the speed and direction of movement (note: this could be a motor planning issue).
Fails to catch himself when falling (could be due to poor body awareness or poorly integrated protective or postural reflexes).
Seem unaware of being moved (e.g. vaguely goes along with the group).
Wonders off or seems unaware of where they are walking.
Have poor spatial awareness and orientation and be easily confused by directions.
Appear lethargic (this student could be often sedentary but can increase alertness when vestibular system is activated e.g. through swinging).
Seems unable to start moving and can therefore appear to be avoiding physical activity (this could also be a planning issue or difficulty initiating/starting movement).

Seeking the zone -
seeking/craving sensory input to calm down or increase alertness or to maintain a focussed state

This student may:
AFSN
Crave certain movement experiences (e.g. jumping, swinging, spinning hopping, bouncing, running).
Seek out gross motor movement and may have a very high tolerance to spinning, jumping, swinging, may not seem to get dizzy.
Pace and walk around classroom or playground (but may be able to sit still if focussed on visually motivating or moving objects because the visual input is activating the vestibular system).
Fidget, rock, squirm or move in chair or shake head and generally have difficulty sitting still in chair or on floor, may need to keep moving (this may interfere with listening and interacting or it may in fact assist the student to listen and be attentive).
Enjoy sensation of falling without regard to safety or playing on edge of furniture, balance on walls or beams.
Like inverted upside down position (e.g. rolls over gym ball or hangs on tummy over swing and hangs head upside down).
Become over- excited by uncontrolled movement activities (e.g. spinning in different directions, summersaults, being chased)
Become over-excited and run when in open space, may appear to want to be chased (this could be a "flight" response).

This checklist is a guide for teachers. Please consult an Occupational Therapist specialising in sensory processing challenges for formal assessment and intervention of your students with complex needs.

LEARNING THROUGH SOUND -AUDITORY PROCESSING

OVER responsive state-
over-registering sensory information, responding with sensitivity, avoiding, defensiveness, fight/flight/fright or freeze
Please note: sound travels through air and bounces off surfaces, changes and distorts. Some students in high alert can over register all these sounds and become sensitive to sound.
Sound also carries a lot of information about different aspects such as: distance (e.g. a bird call), speed (e.g. a motor bike racing past) and size (e.g. a large rumbling truck). these complex aspects can trigger a student's protective system and send them into flight/fight/fright-freeze.

This student may:
AFSN
Experience sensitivity to sound, startle easily or block ears from loud or unexpected noise (e.g. another student vocalising, school bell, fire alarm, microphone feedback, door slamming).
Become distressed or frustrated or find it difficult to pay attention in noisy situations, may avoid noisy environments or places that echo or distort sound.
Tune out sound all together, including the teacher's voice and therefore take longer time to process auditory information.
Have fears of certain sounds and anxiously anticipate them (e.g. microwave or school bell).
Block ears when processing multisensory information or when overwhelmed (e.g. blocks ears when looking at something intently).
Overly notice, be easily distracted or distressed by irrelevant noises in the environment (e.g. air conditioner, footsteps, birds, ticking of clocks, hum of computer, other students).
Increase the volume of their own voice when the volume of the teacher or classroom increases (as a way of blocking out the environmental sounds).
Vocalise, hum or sing to self (this could be helping to block out external noise and keep themselves calm).

UNDER responsive state -
low registration of sensory input, slow or low response, "zoned out"

This student may:
AFSN
Be slow to process auditory input (may need more time to process).
Need words or instructions repeated.
Miss auditory information, take longer to respond to directions or easily lose their place in the conversation.
Not respond to own name.
Have difficulty locating the source or direction of the sound (this could be an issue with discrimination).
Vocalise, hum or sing to self (this could be helping them to stay alert).
Disengage in noisy environments or be unable to participate.

Seeking the zone -
seeking/craving sensory input to calm down or increase alertness or to maintain a focussed state

This student may:
AFSN
Make noise with objects (e.g. bangs, throws, taps objects).
Repeat actions that produce noise (e.g. flush toilet), yell or throw objects.
Vocalise, hum, sing or chat frequently and loudly to provide themselves with sensory input or block out external sounds.
Shout or yell in places that have an echo.
Seek out sounds and music.

This checklist is a guide for teachers. Please consult an Occupational Therapist specialising in sensory processing challenges for formal assessment and intervention of your students with complex needs.

LEARNING THROUGH SIGHT -VISUAL PROCESSING

OVER responsive state-
over-registering sensory information, responding with sensitivity, avoiding, defensiveness, fight/flight/fright or freeze

This student may:
AFSN
Over-notice/be distracted by visual input, e.g. movement of other students, leaves moving in the breeze, notice small changes to the environment or when things are out of place.
Vigilantly watch people or moving objects.
Startle at or avoid moving objects (e.g. a ball or sudden movement)
Be distressed by the sight of moving objects (e.g. battery operated toy).
Have difficulty making eye contact (perhaps distracted by other visual information or can only process one sensory system at a time (e.g. unable to look while listening).
Be sensitive to certain types of light or overly notice changes in light (e.g. moving from indoors to outdoors or through hallways).
Cover eyes, squint or shut eyes.
Look from the corner of his/her eye (perhaps to fix and focus in on image and/or block out other visual sensory information).
Be afraid of the dark (this could be developmental or due to overall anxiety).
Choose dark spaces (this could be because light is overwhelming but dark is soothing).

UNDER responsive state -
low registration of sensory input, slow or low response, "zoned out"
Please note: some of these observations may relate to discrimination of visual information (e.g. recognising details about differences or similarities, colour, shape, size, depth etc.)

This student may:
AFSN
Not seem to "notice" or know where to look.
Fixate on small details but not focus on the task at hand (e.g. pick at fluff on floor rather than focus on the book in front of them).
Look vaguely at objects.
Be distracted by the shine on laminated pictures rather than focus on the picture itself.
Stare out into space.
Miss demonstrations or written/visual information.
Find it difficult or be slow to locate items or words.
Have difficulty with visual discrimination of colour, shape, size, depth.
Bump into objects or people as if they didn't see them.
Have difficulty making eye contact (hasn't noticed or doesn't know where to look).

Seeking the zone -
seeking/craving sensory input to calm down or increase alertness or to maintain a focussed state

This student may:
AFSN
Intently watch spinning or moving objects.
Stare at lights (especially fluorescent lights which can flicker).
Flick objects in front of face.
Look from the corner of their eye (e.g. to fix and focus or process the image differently).
Climb up to look down or get a bird's eye view of the world.
Poke finger in eye or press down on eyelids to focus or distort image.
Stare intently or gets absorbed by patterns or visually motivating items.
Look for or be distracted by own reflection or play with own shadow (this could also be giving the student more information about their body position and movement).
Bring items close to eyes, stares closely at the TV or computer screen or looks through fingers.
Be visually distracted; may notice visual input and movement more than others.
Have difficulty making eye contact or may stare intently into other's faces
Note: difficulty with eye contact is in all 3 categories because it is a complex action that involves integration and modulation of the senses and regulation of alertness levels as well as social-emotional development.

This checklist is a guide for teachers. Please consult an Occupational Therapist specialising in sensory processing challenges for formal assessment and intervention of your students with complex needs.

LEARNING THROUGH TASTE - GUSTATORY PROCESSING AND LEARNING THROUGH SMELL - OLFACTORY PROCESSING

OVER responsive state-
over-registering sensory information, responding with sensitivity, avoiding, defensiveness, fight/flight/fright or freeze

This student may:
AFSN
Be fussy about food: texture and/or taste (e.g. may eat only one type of food). This student may eat a limited variety (e.g. only white foods or only crunchy foods).
Avoid certain tastes or smells or not eat if different foods are touching on the plate (as a protective fright/flight/fight response).
Be afraid of trying new foods
Notice smells and be affected by them more than others
Have a strong gag reflex (e.g. to tastes, smells or textures)

UNDER responsive state -
low registration of sensory input, slow or low response, "zoned out"

This student may:
AFSN
Not notice food left in mouth or around face
Seem to "forget" to chew or swallow.
Have poor saliva control and/or drool (due to poor tactile sensory feedback around mouth and/or low muscle tone).
Not notice smells (e.g. soiled nappy).

Seeking the zone -
seeking/craving sensory input to calm down or increase alertness or to maintain a focussed state

This student may:
AFSN
Stuff mouth with food (this could be for better oral-motor control through enhanced sensory feedback -it's easier to control a large piece of food rather than a tiny crumb in your mouth).
Frequently seek or crave food or something in their mouth (e.g. may chew on collar, clothing).
Lick, taste or mouth non-food items (e.g. pencils, plastic, rocks, sand, leaves). This could be a form of exploration or be related to a Biomedical issue or mineral imbalance such as a zinc or magnesium deficiency.
Seek out some smells, notices smells more than others.
May be drawn to certain smells more than others (e.g. body odour, shampoo).
Crave mouthing items, eating certain flavours or textures (this could be to ALERT themselves).
Explore items, places or people by smelling them.
Note: Mouthing can remain the primary mode of exploration and learning for some students who are diagnosed with developmental delay.

This checklist is a guide for teachers. Please consult an Occupational Therapist specialising in sensory processing challenges for formal assessment and intervention of your students with complex needs.
